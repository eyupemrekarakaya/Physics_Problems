## 1. Gravitational Dependence

For a simple pendulum, the period is given by:

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

### (a) Period on the Moon

Since the length of the pendulum does not change, the period is inversely proportional to the square root of gravitational acceleration:

$$
T \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}
$$

On the Moon, the gravitational acceleration is approximately:

$$
g_{\text{Moon}} = \frac{g_{\text{Earth}}}{6}
$$

So the new period becomes:

$$
T_{\text{Moon}} = T_{\text{Earth}} \sqrt{\frac{g_{\text{Earth}}}{g_{\text{Moon}}}}
$$

Substituting the values:

$$
T_{\text{Moon}} = 4\sqrt{6}
$$

$$
T_{\text{Moon}} \approx 4 \times 2.45 = 9.8 \text{ s}
$$

**Answer:** The period on the Moon would be approximately **9.8 s**.

### (b) Required length for a period of 1 second on Earth

Using the pendulum formula:

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

Solve for $L$:

$$
L = \frac{gT^2}{4\pi^2}
$$

Substitute $T = 1 \text{ s}$ and $g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$:

$$
L = \frac{9.8(1)^2}{4\pi^2}
$$

$$
L = \frac{9.8}{39.48}
$$

$$
L \approx 0.248 \text{ m}
$$

**Answer:** The required length is approximately **0.248 m** (or **24.8 cm**).
